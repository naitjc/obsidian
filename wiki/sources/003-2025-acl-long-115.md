# HATEDAY: Insights from a Global Hate Speech Dataset Representative of a Day on Twitter

## Metadata
- Source file: `raw/sources/2025.acl-long.115.pdf`
- Year: 2025
- Pages: 25
- Ingest level: deep-ingest-v2 (multi-section extraction)

## Problem Framing
- Addresses the gap between academic hate-speech benchmarks and real-world social media moderation settings.
- Highlights that existing evaluations often overestimate model performance because datasets are biased or narrow.
- Emphasizes multilingual and cross-country evaluation gaps in online hate speech detection.

## Method
- Introduces HATEDAY, a dataset sampled from all tweets posted on September 21, 2022.
- Constructs representative samples across eight languages and four English-speaking countries.
- Evaluates publicly available hate-speech detection models and compares automatic moderation with human-in-the-loop scenarios.

## Data and Evaluation Setup
- HATEDAY contains 240,000 annotated tweets, with 20,000 tweets per language/country setting.
- The dataset supports cross-lingual and cross-national comparison of hate prevalence, targets, and model performance.
- Evaluation focuses on real-world Twitter distributions rather than curated academic benchmark distributions.

## Results and Claims
- Finds that hate speech has low prevalence in representative Twitter samples.
- Shows that public hate-speech models perform poorly in real-world settings, especially for non-European languages.
- Argues that high moderation rates are only achievable with substantial human oversight.

## Limitations and Follow-ups
- The study is tied to one sampled day on Twitter and may not capture temporal spikes or platform changes.
- Moderation conclusions depend on the public models and thresholds evaluated.
- Verify exact prevalence, model scores, and sampling details before citing quantitative conclusions.
